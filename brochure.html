Assessing HIV Status Among High School and College Students in Relation to HIV Prevention

Knowing your HIV status is an important step toward protecting your health.

Target Audience:

In the pretest, five high school and college students (ages 16‚Äì21) from a local youth center were used. A copy of the brochure was printed, and each participant answered the following questions separately.

1. What is the first thing that caught your eye when you first saw this piece?

The participants were told that at first they paid attention to the name Know Your HIV Status and smiling students on campus. Their design was characterized as modern, clean, and easy to read, which is suitable for teens and younger adults.

2. What do you think its main message is?

Every participant accurately described that the brochure uses language to prompt the youth to be tested for HIV and appreciate why it is important to know the status of their health and safety. According to them, the message was direct and well articulated.

3. Does it raise your interest in the subject?

Yes. According to students, the brochure made them more interested in learning more about HIV, testing, and prevention. Others said that the brochure decreased confusion or fear regarding HIV testing and made the subject more comfortable.

4. Was the information easy to understand?

Participants agreed that the brochure was written in clear and simple language. The layout and headings helped them quickly understand the key message without feeling overwhelmed.

5. Would you recommend this brochure to others?

Most students responded positively and stated that they would share the brochure with friends because it provides helpful and important health information.

Summary of Recommended Changes Based on Pretest

Participants recommended that the font size be enlarged and that the line spacing be made larger to facilitate easier reading before final distribution. Their other suggestions were to have a pastel background, reduce glare, include a local mental-health contact number, and have an inspiring quote like ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs never too late to take care of your mind.‚Äù In general, the brochure was reported to be clear, appealing, and encouraging to the aged adults, who indicated that they would be happy distributing it to those within their age group.

Key Recommendations:

  1. Increase font size slightly for easier readability.
  2. Add more spacing between lines and bullet points for visual comfort.
  3. Use a light beige or pastel background instead of white to reduce glare.
  4. Include a local mental-health contact number or resource for quick help.
  5. Add an encouraging quote such as ‚ÄúKnowing your status gives you confidence and control.‚Äù

On the whole, the participants were satisfied with the clarity, motivation, and appearance of the brochure, as well as declared their readiness to share it with their peers in their age group.

Conclusion

The pretest findings reveal that the message, tone, and design of the brochure are the right combination for the high school and college students. Minor visual and layout improvements are needed. The brochure effectively generates awareness in relation to HIV testing and supports the cognitive learning objective, which is influencing positive health decisions among the young people.
