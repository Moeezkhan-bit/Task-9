Intervention Program Plan

1. Title of Project

Assessing HIV Status Among High School and College Students in Relation to HIV Prevention

2. Author

FNU Unseba

3. Problem / Need Statement

Young people aged 15‚Äì24 in Cook County have low HIV testing rates, stigma, and limited access to youth-friendly confidential services. Fear, misinformation, and low perceived risk delay diagnosis and increase community spread. A targeted health communication intervention is necessary to increase awareness and normalize regular testing.

4. Goal

To increase the number of adolescents and young adults who get tested regularly by reducing stigma, improving awareness, and providing culturally relevant and accessible testing resources.

Goal 2

To enhance the general health literacy on HIV among youth by correcting myths, reducing stigma, and making testing a normal and routine part of healthy living.

5. Objectives

For Goal 1:

  • Increase the number of youth who can name at least two local locations that provide free and confidential HIV testing by 30% within six months.
  • Raise voluntary HIV testing participation to 20% through partner clinics, on-campus testing events, and mobile testing units within 12 months.

For Goal 2:

  • Increase knowledge of HIV transmission, risk, and testing instructions among students by 25% based on pre- and post-intervention surveys.
  • Reduce perceived HIV stigma such as fear, embarrassment, or shame by 15% after campaign exposure.

6. Sponsoring Agency / Contact Person

Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH)
HIV/STI Prevention Program Coordinator

7. Primary Target Audience(s)

High school and college students ages 15‚Äì24 in Cook County.

8. Primary Audience Key Strategies

(Audience ‚Üí Action/Message ‚Üí Barriers ‚Üí Benefits ‚Üí Credentials ‚Üí Channels)

Audience:

Youth in high school and college who are active digital media users within Cook County and whose lifestyles rely on peer influence and mobile-based communications are aged 15 to 24.

Action/Message:

Promote youth volunteering by encouraging them to undergo testing as a means to get results and take control of their health by promoting HIV testing using the message:

‚ÄúKnow your status. Free, fast, confidential HIV testing is available near you. Taking control of your health is a smart, responsible, and empowering decision.‚Äù

The messages emphasize that testing is easy, confidential, noninvasive, and offered at places that understand and respect youth concerns.

Barriers:

Youth may face fear of peer or adult judgment, uncertainty about confidentiality, low perceived risk, lack of knowledge about testing locations, and cultural or family stigma. Some may also worry about the impact of a positive result or misunderstand HIV transmission.

Benefits:

Testing provides peace of mind, empowerment, early identification, and timely treatment. It promotes responsible health behavior and ensures privacy and confidentiality while supporting healthier relationships and stronger communities.

Credentials:

Messages will be endorsed by the Chicago Department of Public Health, local health professionals, school-based health centers, youth peer ambassadors, and trusted community organizations. These reliable and familiar sources increase credibility and trust among youth.

9. Secondary Target Audience(s)

  • Parents & caregivers: Influence attitudes and provide support for youth health decisions.
  • School administrators & health educators: Key gatekeepers who enable school-based testing access and prevention programs.
  • Community youth organizations: Provide safe spaces for outreach, education, and health promotion activities.

10. Secondary Audience Key Strategies

Audience:

Parents & Caregivers

Action/Message:

Build their case about the need to help their teens receive accurate HIV information and regular testing as preventive health measures. Emphasize that HIV can potentially affect any youth regardless of background and that early diagnosis leads to better long-term health outcomes.

Barriers:

Cultural hesitation in discussing sexual health, assumptions that their child is not sexually active, stigma, and limited knowledge about HIV and testing services.

Benefits:

Protects the long-term health of their child, enables early treatment if needed, and promotes supportive and open communication within families.

Credentials:

CDPH officers, pediatricians, and school nurses.

Channels:

Parent newsletters, PTA meetings, informational flyers, community workshops, and Facebook groups.
