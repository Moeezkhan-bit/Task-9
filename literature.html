Literature Review

Assessing HIV Status Among High School and College Students in Relation to HIV Prevention

Introduction

This review is a study of HIV status among high school and college students with a focus on its relationship with prevention behavior. It identifies stigma, low awareness, and poor communication as obstacles to testing and protective action. The proposed intervention, Know Your Status, Protect Your Future, is to enhance HIV literacy and reduce stigmas through peer education and digital campaigning, and promote voluntary testing (Reinius, Eriksson, Svedhem, & Esfahani, 2021).

The review is divided into three parts: the introduction, where the problem and intervention are defined, the body, where evidence is summarized by theme, and the conclusion, where the research findings are contextualized to the proposed communications strategy to improve youth HIV prevention.

Literature Review

Theme 1: Knowledge, Attitudes, and Perceptions of HIV Among Students

According to Harris (2019), many students still believe in the falsity of HIV transmission and prevention. The study indicates medium awareness and low knowledge of the importance of testing. Some believe that only specific groups are at risk due to HIV; thus, their chances of getting infected are less. Girls are more educated and less outspoken in demanding testing. Cultural silence on sexual health perpetuates common misinformation. Overall, the lack of knowledge and ambiguity toward students invalidates their wish to test or accept preventive behavior.

This shows that it is important that we have a comprehensive HIV education where all the myths will be busted and the students will be encouraged to make the right health choices (Harris, 2019). Peer and open conversation and culturally sensitive communication should be included in educational programs in order to form more awareness and motivate the youth to undergo voluntary testing to eventually result in a more responsible and health-conscious youth.

Theme 2: Barriers to HIV Testing and Status Disclosure

According to Khamisa (2020), barriers to youth engagement include a lack of youth-friendly services and a lack of confidentiality in school-based clinics. There are also cultural taboos related to sex, and when they reflect the status, then it is passed on to the testing, where an individual will be unsure about the result and fearful of the reaction of others. The barriers are less collaborative transaction styles, restricted, accommodating, and limited.

In this respect, it has been recommended that educational institutions and health care organizations must provide testing pathways that are both confidential and youth-oriented, thus providing them with privacy and emotional security (Khamisa, Mokgobi, & Basera, 2020). HIV should be de-stigmatized and normalized to discuss and test HIV, understanding that peer-education and peer-counselors, one person can access to create stronger awareness. A setting where it is stigmatized will increase the number of tests and encourage young people that they can choose how to live their sexual life.

Theme 3: Effectiveness of Communication and Educational Interventions

According to Patrick (2024), Interactive education among young people has been reported to produce improved knowledge about HIV and behavior on testing. Students who are especially inclined to communicate with the help of campaigns using the power of social media will learn without difficulty. Of course, this has been supported by a theoretically-informed presence, including the Theory of Planned Behavior and the Health Behavior Theory, now that knowledge brings intention.

Most successful interventions tend to be factual in nature with minimal stigmatizing and affective aspects. The assumption of long-term contact is culturally competent, recognizable communication (Patrick, 2024). It is prudent to consider the new and transformational opportunities in communication with students that offer learning and credibility in action in light of increased contact. A more interactive form of information and peer education can be provided through technology. The change of behaviour and the development of awareness of HIV in the educational environment is created through regular participation and behaviour change over time, contingent upon the cultural and social reality of students.

Theme 4: Policy and Institutional Support for HIV Prevention in Schools

According to Ibrahim (2024), policies to promote HIV education are implemented unequally in schools and colleges. Many institutions lack trained teachers and a standardization of practice. Another crucial support to prevention programs is education and health ministry support, which has to be coordinated and financed. With proper policy guidelines, the introduction of HIV education in schools as a school health curriculum makes a positive contribution to the awareness level.

Both institutional commitment and communication-based approaches justify the long-term impact and accountability on student HIV prevention (Ibrahim, Kahle, Christiani, & Suryani, 2024). Consistent HIV education is important to increasing policy coordination, educator funding, and training. The sustainability, responsibility, and overall effectiveness of communication practices in schools would minimize the risk of HIV among students.

Summary and Conclusions

The reviewed studies suggest that knowledge deficiency, stigmatization, and low institutional support are barriers to student prevention of HIV. Effective communication, especially peer-led, digital, and culturally sensitive communication, improves knowledge and testing behaviour. However, inconsistent policy implementation and unqualified teachers are issues.

The Know Your Status, Protect Your Future intervention aims to address these gaps through the establishment of easy-to-access and youth-friendly education and confidential testing. The only way that HIV prevention among youths can be sustainable is by intensifying communication and policy coordination in schools.
