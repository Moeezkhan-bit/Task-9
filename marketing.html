MARKETING PLAN

PH 430: Health Literacy & Communication

Topic: Assessing HIV Status Among High School and College Students

I. Executive Overview

A. Introduction and Purpose

This marketing plan suggests the creation of a youth-focused health communication intervention that will raise HIV status awareness and encourage HIV testing in high school and college students.

B. Need for the Intervention

Students in high schools and colleges aged between 15 and 24 constitute the priority population of this intervention.

C. Description of the Proposed Intervention

Goals

  • Behavior: Test high school and college students voluntarily more often.
  • Actions: Get students to campus testing events, home tests, and local clinics.
  • Key Influencers: PEs, Campus health centers, school leaders, parents, and social media influencers.
  • Audience: Young people between 15 and 24 years attending high schools, community colleges, and universities.

Objectives

  1. Objective 1 (Short-term): 12 months later, the program will boost HIV knowledge scores by 30% among participants.
  2. Objective 2 (Behavioral): Within the context of the program, 20% more participating students will increase their HIV tests by the end of the program.
  3. Objective 3 (Environmental): Form collaborations with five local clinics to provide free or low-cost youth-friendly testing.

2. Overview of the Project

The work is proposed and referred to as Know Your Status: Youth HIV Prevention campaign that involves digital communications, school-based outreach, and available testing options. Some strategies are social media messaging, educational workshops, peer-led sessions, and mobile testing campuses.

II. The Customers (Market Review)

A. Primary Target Audience

The target market is high school and college students, and by analyzing their behavior, culture, demographics, physical considerations, and even psychographics, they can be characterized.

  • On the behavioral level, this group is active in digital communication, has high dependency on social media to inform, and can also be involved in sexual activity.
  • The group is culturally heterogeneous, as it covers a great variety of ethnic, linguistic, and family backgrounds.
  • The demographics of this audience are adolescents and young adults registered in secondary or post-secondary institutions.
  • Physically, the target population consists of all genders and sexual orientations having different levels of exposure to sexual health risks.
  • Psychographically, the audience attaches importance to independence, social affiliation, and personal image.

B. Secondary Target Audience

The secondary audience will comprise parents, school administrators, teachers, community health workers, and campus health center personnel. These people shape students with their policies, education, and resources. They must assist in the creation of a normal environment of HIV testing where it is accessible and stigma-free.

IV. Strategies

Position Description
Position The campaign will frame HIV testing as a normal, responsible, and empowering decision rather than something associated with stigma or fear.
Product Free testing events, confidential services, peer education programs, and accessible digital health materials.
Price The program is free for students and funded through grants and institutional health budgets.
Promotion Promotion will occur through school emails, campus media, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube using youth-friendly messaging.

BEHAVE Framework

Target Audience Action Benefits & Barriers Activities

Who?

A specific target audience

To help:

High school and college students ages 15--24

#1. Know exactly who your audience is and look at everything from their point of view.

What?

Take a specific action.

To:

Get tested for HIV (campus events, clinics, or home-test kits)

#2. Your Bottom Line: When all is said and done, the audience's action is what counts.

Benefits & Barriers

Benefits and barriers that influence the action

We will focus on:

Benefits:

  • Free & confidential
  • Peace of mind
  • Early detection improves health
  • Encouragement from peers
  • Feel responsible & empowered

Barriers:

  • Stigma/fear of judgment
  • Fear of positive results
  • Believe they are "low risk"
  • Don't know where to get tested
  • Privacy concerns
  • Misinformation

#3. If it benefits them, they'll take an action. Barriers keep them from acting.

Activities

Selected program activities that address these benefits and barriers

Through:

  • Peer educator workshops
  • Mobile on-campus HIV testing
  • Social media campaigns (TikTok/Instagram)
  • Youth influencers promoting testing
  • School-based HIV education sessions
  • Partnerships with clinics for free/low-cost testing.

#4. All your activities should maximize the benefits and minimize the barriers that matter to the target audience.
